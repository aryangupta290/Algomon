Recursion

Hey there trainee! Welcome to another challenge. Today's battle is very interesting. In today's battle, you will be given few stones with various weights. There are multiple stones available corresponding to the weights. Various weights available are given. The person who reaches a target weight in minimum number of stones used wins the game. Your opponent is a strong player, however, after observing his games, we have found out the way he tries to fill stones. Since you are the winner of previous rounds, you will be given the privilege to choose the target weight. Your task is to figure out a weight for which your opponent may fail, and you should also device an algorithm to always find the best possible answer for any given n-target weight.

Your opponent's algorithm is as follows:

Let n be the target weight and w be the weight on stones added so far. Select the stone with largest weight that can be added such that sum of weight of stone and w doesn't exceed n. For example: If available stone weights are 10,5,2,1 and n = 19, He would first select 10. then 5. Then the stone with weight 2 twice and then the stone with weight 1. In this manner your opponent is able to achieve the weight 19 in exactly 5 stones(10+5+2+2+1.

Let us figure out a way to win this battle as well. It is crucial to first understand why the opponent's strategy doesn't work. If you haven't thought about it yet, kindly give it a thought, and try to figure out a case when the opponent's strategy fails. Let us consider the case when n = 18 and the available weights are {10,6,4,1} According to the opponent's strategy, he would first choose the stone with weight 10 followed by stone of weight 6 and then 2 stones of weight 1. However, the optimal selection involves selecting 10+4+4 with only 3 stones.

So what should be the optimal strategy then? Well it turns out that in order to be always assured of making the best choice, you should try all possible combinations. But how do we go about formalizing a method such that we cover all combinations in any general case?

Here we learn about the recursion technique.

We formulate a method to obtain the minimum number of stones to reach the target weight. In this algorithm, at each step we consider all possible stones. Although this technique is slow, we are assured to get the best answer. We can speed up this process later when we learn about the dynamic programming technique to optimize the recursive algorithms. Let us start with weight 0. At each step, we add all possible weights and check the best option. For example, here we have 10,6,4,1 as possible stone weights. From 0, we can get to sum of weights as 10,6,4,1 and from each of these, we can add weights {10,6,4,1} again and so on. But when do we stop this process? Recursive algorithms have the concept of base case. In a recursive algorithm, a base case is the one after which no matter what other possibilities we try, we can't reach to the optimal state. In our example, let us assume the sum of weights of stone becomes greater than 18(the target value). In this case, we can't reach 18 whatever we try since we do not have stones with negative weight. Hence all cases where sum of weights is greater than target weight is a base case and we stop the recursive process after reaching this step.

A recursive algorithm is exhaustive. At every step, we divide the set of solutions into 2 sets. One set has the solutions where the stone with a given weight is included in the optimal answer, and the other set has the set of solutions in which that stone isn't included.

Thus in this way we can obtain the optimal answer for every target weight n.
